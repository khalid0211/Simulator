## Summary

The Portfolio Simulator places you in the role of a portfolio manager in a provincial government. Over 60 simulated months you must select, fund, and deliver capital projects from a pool of 30 candidates, using a budget that is deliberately insufficient to complete all of them. The exercise requires you to make and defend the kinds of decisions that define effective public investment management in practice: which projects to start, when to start them, how to respond when costs rise or funding falls short, and how to balance strategic alignment against delivery pressure.

The simulation is not a test of technical knowledge. It is a test of judgement. It models the real pressures that portfolio managers face — irregular fund releases, inflation eroding the value of delay, mandatory political commitments consuming strategic space, approval processes adding lag to decisions, and cost and schedule shocks emerging at critical project milestones. Your performance is assessed against three dimensions: how many projects you deliver, how well those projects align with strategic priorities, and how efficiently you deploy the budget available to you.

This briefing sets out the context, the mechanics, and the evaluation criteria. Read it before you start. Return to it during the exercise if you need to reorient.

## 1  Introduction and purpose

**1.** This exercise forms part of the Public Investment Management (PIM) training programme developed under the Punjab PRIDE Programme. It is designed to build practical decision-making capability in portfolio and programme management for officials of the Planning and Development Board (P&DB) and line departments of the Government of Punjab.

**2.** The simulation draws on the governance and financial management principles set out in the Punjab Development Manual 2026, including the project lifecycle stages, portfolio monitoring framework, and the Annual Development Programme (ADP) budgeting process. Participants who have completed the Economic and Financial Appraisal Guidelines and the Earned Value Management Practical Guide will recognise many of the underlying concepts in operational form.

**3.** The exercise has two complementary objectives. First, it develops intuition for portfolio-level cash flow management — a skill that is difficult to acquire through instruction alone but becomes clear through the consequences of decisions made under resource pressure. Second, it surfaces the strategic trade-offs inherent in public portfolio management: the tension between delivering many projects and delivering the right projects, and between maintaining commitments and adapting to changing circumstances.

---

## 2  The real-world setting

**4.** The simulation is set in a provincial government managing its capital development portfolio. The context is deliberately recognisable to Punjab officials, though the specific project names and figures are generated randomly for each run.

**5.** Punjab's ADP represents one of the largest sub-national development budgets in Pakistan, covering infrastructure, social services, agriculture, and governance reform. In any given year, the P&DB manages hundreds of active schemes across all sectors, with total portfolio demand routinely exceeding available budget by a factor of 1.5 to 2. This over-subscription is not a planning failure — it reflects the legitimate competition of priority needs against a finite resource envelope. The skill of portfolio management lies in navigating that competition intelligently.

**6.** Several features of Punjab's actual ADP process are directly replicated in the simulation. Budget allocations are released quarterly rather than as a lump sum, replicating the fiscal year release patterns of provincial Finance Departments. Projects cannot be initiated without completing an approval process — the simulation models this as an approval lag. Inflation erodes the value of both undeployed funds and the budgets of un-started projects. And a proportion of the portfolio is, in practice, not freely selectable — political and policy commitments pre-occupy a share of available capacity and budget, regardless of strategic merit.

**7.** The simulation compresses these dynamics into a 60-month horizon precisely because the consequences of poor decisions — starting the wrong projects, hoarding cash, over-committing capacity — are often not visible until months or years after the decisions are taken. The exercise makes those consequences visible within the duration of a training session.

## 3  Your role and mandate

**8.** You are the Portfolio Manager for a provincial government's capital development portfolio. Your mandate has three components.

**9.** First, you are responsible for **strategic delivery**: ensuring that the portfolio produces the greatest possible contribution to the government's development priorities within the available budget. This means selecting projects with the highest strategic alignment and completing as many of them as the budget allows.

**10.** Second, you are responsible for **financial stewardship**: deploying the budget efficiently, without waste through abandoned schemes or idle unspent balances, and without over-commitment that triggers funding shortfalls.

**11.** Third, you are responsible for **portfolio governance**: making timely decisions — when to start, when to slow, when to suspend, and on rare occasions when to abandon — based on accurate reading of the portfolio's financial and delivery position.

**12.** You have authority over all projects in the portfolio except those designated as political commitments, which are subject to a mandatory protection period. Within that constraint, all decisions are yours to make and to defend.

## 4  The portfolio challenge

**13.** At the start of each run, 30 projects are generated with varying budgets, durations, strategic alignment scores, and risk profiles. The total budget available to you over 60 months is set at approximately two-thirds of what it would cost to complete all 30 projects. You can, in principle, complete around 17 to 25 of them, depending on which difficulty setting you are running.

**14.** This scarcity is intentional and realistic. ADP portfolios are structurally over-subscribed. The task is not to find a way to complete everything — that is not possible. The task is to make the best possible use of the budget available, which requires active prioritisation rather than passive administration.

**15.** The fundamental tension in the exercise is between volume and quality. The more projects you complete, the higher your delivery score. But the scoring also rewards the strategic alignment of the projects you choose to complete. A portfolio of ten completed but low-alignment projects will score less well than a portfolio of eight completed high-alignment projects. The optimal strategy lies in completing as many high-alignment projects as the budget allows — which is a selection, sequencing, and cash flow management problem simultaneously.

**16.** A second tension concerns timing. Projects that remain unstarted inflate in cost every month. Idle budget is penalised as waste in the final score. Both pressures argue for deploying capital early. However, starting too many projects simultaneously strains monthly cash flow and increases the risk of funding shortfalls that force costly slowdowns or suspensions. Effective portfolio management requires balancing the cost of delay against the cost of over-commitment.

## 5  How the simulation works

**17.** The simulation runs for 60 monthly cycles. At the start of each month, you review the portfolio state — which projects are active, how they are progressing, what the funding position is — and take any actions you choose. When you are ready, you advance to the next month. The exercise ends at Month 60 and your performance score is displayed.

**The monthly cycle.** Each month proceeds in the following sequence. Pending projects awaiting approval are automatically activated when their approval lag expires. Quarterly budget tranches are released at the start of Months 1, 4, 7, and so on through to Month 58. The budgets of all unstarted projects increase slightly due to inflation. Risk events may fire if any active project crosses a spending milestone. The system then checks whether your active projects' combined funding demand exceeds your available balance: if it does, a shortfall resolution panel appears and you must take action before advancing. After resolving any shortfall, the month's spending is deducted, project progress is updated, and any projects reaching completion are recorded. You then enter the decision phase, where you may add new projects, adjust the pace of existing ones, or take other actions. Pressing Advance moves to the next month.

**18.** Projects follow a **Beta distribution S-curve** spending profile — spending is slow in the early months, accelerates through the middle of the project life, then tapers as the project approaches completion. This reflects the natural pattern of capital project expenditure: design and procurement dominate the early period, construction and commissioning the middle, and defects rectification and handover the tail. You will see this shape displayed on the S-Curves tab of the dashboard.

**19.** The **Cash Flow tab** shows the projected aggregate monthly spending requirement against the projected available balance for all 60 months. This is your primary strategic planning tool. Months where the red line (required spending) approaches or crosses the green line (available funds) are early warnings of upcoming pressure. Acting on these warnings two to three months in advance — by voluntarily slowing a low-priority project — is consistently less costly than waiting for the shortfall panel to force your hand.

**20.** The dashboard also provides a **Gantt chart** of all portfolio projects, with their current state, milestone markers, and projected completion months. Projects projected to complete after Month 60 are flagged with a warning indicator. The **KPI tab** shows a live estimate of your projected final score, updated after each advance.

## 6  Actions available to you

**21.** You have six actions available at each monthly decision point.

**Add to portfolio.** You select a project from the available pool and commit it to the portfolio. Its budget is locked at the current inflation-adjusted figure — waiting makes it more expensive. If an approval lag is configured, the project enters a pending state and begins drawing funds only after the lag expires. If a concurrent project cap is in place, you may not add a project if the cap is already reached.

**Speed up.** You compress a project's remaining schedule by a percentage you choose (between 5% and 60%). This raises the monthly spending requirement and incurs a crash premium — an additional cost of 50% of the compression percentage applied to the remaining budget. For example, a 40% compression increases the remaining budget by 20% and roughly doubles the monthly burn. Speed-up is a late-game rescue tool for near-complete projects at risk of expiring; it is too expensive to use routinely.

**Slow down.** You extend a project's remaining schedule by a percentage you choose. The same remaining budget is re-spread over more months, lowering the monthly spending requirement. This is the primary lever for managing cash flow pressure. A 50% slowdown roughly halves the monthly burn and doubles the remaining duration. Slowing a project slightly increases its total real cost through additional inflation months, but this is almost always less costly than the alternatives.

**Suspend.** You pause a project entirely, removing its monthly spending requirement until you resume it. Suspension is a stronger measure than slowing: it eliminates demand completely but triggers a 10% remaining-duration penalty on resumption. The undrawn remaining budget continues to inflate while the project is on hold, so suspension is rarely the most economical response to a cash flow squeeze. Prefer slowing unless you need to zero out a project's demand completely.

**Resume.** You restart a suspended project. A duration penalty of 10% of the remaining schedule (rounded up to the nearest month) is added automatically, and the project is rebaselined over the extended duration.

**Abandon.** You permanently remove a project from the portfolio. All expenditure to date is lost. A penalty of two points is deducted from your final score, and the sunk cost contributes to your budget waste ratio. Abandonment is almost never the right choice for a project more than 40% complete — the sunk cost plus the score penalty will in nearly all cases exceed the cost of completing the project, even slowly. Abandon only projects in their early stages when financial pressure is severe and the strategic alignment of the project is weak.

**22.** You also have access to two advisory tools at any point. The **Hint button** provides a context-aware coaching recommendation based on the current portfolio state — it names specific projects and states the financial reasoning. It is a coaching prompt, not an instruction; the judgement remains yours. The **Report button** generates a structured portfolio status report analysing your current position, decisions taken to date, and recommended actions for the next five months.

## 7  Real-world factors modelled in this exercise

**23.** The simulation models eight features of real public portfolio management that are frequently underweighted in classroom instruction.

**Inflation and the cost of delay.** In the simulation, every month that a project remains unstarted, its budget increases by the monthly inflation rate. At 5% annual inflation, a Rs 1 billion project costs approximately Rs 25 million more after six months of inaction. This effect compounds: the longer the delay, the more of the budget envelope is consumed before the first rupee is deployed. This reflects the genuine opportunity cost of slow ADP utilisation — a problem that consistently reduces the real value of Punjab's development programme.

**Irregular funding release patterns.** Government budget releases are rarely smooth. In many fiscal years, the Finance Department releases a small first-quarter allocation, the bulk of funds in the third quarter, and a supplementary tranche in the fourth. The simulation models five release patterns — flat, S-curve, front-loaded, back-loaded, and volatile — each of which creates different cash flow management challenges. A back-loaded release pattern, for example, means that projects started early in the year face a period of thin funding before the main tranche arrives, requiring the portfolio manager to maintain adequate cash reserves or stagger project starts accordingly.

**Budget scarcity and tightening.** The ADP is structurally over-subscribed. The budget tightness parameter in the simulation replicates this: at the standard setting, completing all projects would require 1.5 times the available budget. At the severe setting, it requires 2.2 times. The practical implication is the same in both the simulation and reality — active prioritisation is unavoidable. The discipline required is not simply to select the best projects; it is to select the combination of projects that collectively delivers the most strategic value within the budget constraint.

**Political and mandatory project commitments.** A proportion of the ADP portfolio in any given year comprises schemes that have been committed through political decisions, constituency-level negotiations, or legislative mandates. These schemes may not reflect the highest strategic priorities, but they cannot be removed from the portfolio without significant political cost. The simulation models this through political projects: a number of low-alignment schemes are forced into the portfolio at the start of the run, locked against abandonment for 24 months, and consuming budget that would otherwise be available for higher-priority selection. Managing around this constraint — allocating the remaining budget to the highest-alignment discretionary projects — is a core skill for any public portfolio manager.

**Institutional capacity constraints.** Delivering capital projects is not merely a financial challenge; it also requires the institutional capacity to supervise, manage, and absorb them. Departments can only effectively manage a limited number of active projects simultaneously. When too many projects are initiated at once, supervision quality falls, delays increase, and the risk of cost overruns rises. The concurrent project cap in the simulation replicates this constraint. Even without a hard cap, the financial dynamics of the simulation penalise over-commitment: spreading a thin budget across too many active projects simultaneously lowers the monthly burn on each one, extending their durations, increasing their exposure to inflation, and raising the risk that several expire simultaneously at the end of the simulation period.

**Approval and procurement lag.** In Punjab's actual project cycle, a project does not begin spending on construction the day it receives ADP allocation. It must complete the PC-I approval process, conduct procurement, and award contracts before physical work begins. This typically takes two to six months, during which the project absorbs administrative capacity without delivering progress. The approval lag parameter in the simulation replicates this: projects added to the portfolio enter a pending state and begin drawing funds only after the lag expires. The practical implication is that the portfolio manager must plan starts further in advance than a purely financial analysis would suggest, and must account for the pipeline of pending projects when assessing available capacity.

**Project risk and cost/schedule volatility.** Capital projects routinely encounter cost and schedule shocks at key delivery milestones. Ground conditions, supply chain disruptions, scope changes, and design errors are among the most common causes. The simulation fires risk events at three points in each project's life — when cumulative spending crosses 25%, 50%, and 75% of the project budget — and independently rolls for cost increases or reductions and duration extensions or compressions. The risk environment parameter scales the probability and magnitude of these events. Under the turbulent setting, cost and schedule shocks are both more frequent and larger, requiring the portfolio manager to maintain greater contingency and to respond more actively to rebaseline alerts.

**The cost of idling capital.** One of the less intuitive insights from the exercise is that unspent budget at the end of the run is penalised in exactly the same way as money wasted on abandoned projects. This reflects a genuine principle of public financial management: budget that is allocated but not deployed has an opportunity cost equivalent to its unspent value. It represents development activity that was authorised but not delivered. In the simulation, as in the ADP, the objective is not to preserve a comfortable cash reserve — it is to maximise the development impact of every rupee allocated.

## 8  How you will be evaluated

**24.** Your performance is assessed at the end of Month 60 using a composite score out of 100 points, comprising three components and a penalty mechanism.

**Delivery Score — 40 points.** This component measures the ratio of projects you completed to the maximum number of projects that the available budget could theoretically fund. The maximum is computed at the start of each run by identifying how many of the cheapest available projects could be completed within the total budget. Your delivery score equals (projects completed / maximum possible) × 40, capped at 40 points. A run in which you complete 16 projects where 20 were theoretically possible earns 32 delivery points; completing exactly the maximum earns all 40.

**Strategic Alignment Score — 35 points.** This component measures the average strategic alignment score of the projects you completed. Alignment is scored on a scale of 0.20 to 1.00 for each project. The average alignment of your completed projects is multiplied by 35. A portfolio of completed projects with average alignment of 0.85 earns approximately 30 alignment points; a portfolio averaging 0.55 earns approximately 19 points. Note that only completed projects count: a high-alignment project that expires at Month 60 contributes nothing to this component and incurs the expiry penalty.

**Budget Efficiency Score — 25 points.** This component measures how well you deployed the available budget. The "wasted budget ratio" is computed as the sum of sunk costs on abandoned projects plus any unspent balance at Month 60, divided by the total portfolio budget. Your efficiency score equals (1 − wasted ratio) × 25. A run with no abandonments and no idle balance at the end earns the full 25 points. A run in which 10% of the total budget was either abandoned or unspent earns approximately 22.5 points.

**Penalties.** Each project you abandon deducts two points from the composite total. Each project that expires at Month 60 — still active, suspended, or pending but incomplete — deducts one point. These penalties are subtracted after the three components are summed, and the total score is floored at zero.

**25.** The following table shows the performance bands and their descriptors.

| Score | Band | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|
| 85–100 | Excellent | Exceptional portfolio management demonstrating mastery of selection, sequencing, and financial stewardship |
| 70–84 | Good | Sound decision-making with minor inefficiencies in selection or capital deployment |
| 55–69 | Satisfactory | Adequate delivery but with identifiable weaknesses in prioritisation or cash flow management |
| 40–54 | Poor | Significant inefficiencies observed — over-commitment, poor selection, or substantial undeployed budget |
| 0–39 | Needs Development | Fundamental gaps in portfolio decision-making, typically characterised by mass expiries, large abandonments, or persistently idle cash |

**26.** At the end of each run, the debrief screen shows your score breakdown, a full timeline of decisions taken, and a comparison of your delivered portfolio against an **optimal benchmark portfolio**. The benchmark is computed by selecting projects in order of strategic alignment per unit of budget until the total budget is exhausted — it represents the theoretically optimal selection if all projects could be started simultaneously with no cash flow constraints. Your score relative to the benchmark illustrates the gap between what was achievable in principle and what was achieved in practice, and helps identify whether the shortfall arose from selection choices, timing decisions, or financial management.

## 9  Key principles before you start

**27.** The following principles distil the experience of multiple simulation runs and reflect established practice in public portfolio management. They are offered not as rules to follow mechanically but as starting points for your own thinking.

**Deploy capital early.** Delay is always costly. Every month a project remains unstarted, its budget increases and your idle balance counts against efficiency. Within the constraints of the concurrent cap and the approval lag, aim to start the highest-priority affordable projects as early as possible.

**Select by strategic value per unit of cost.** The primary ranking criterion for new project selection should be alignment divided by budget — the strategic return per rupee. This single metric simultaneously optimises both the delivery score and the alignment score, and it is the basis on which the optimal benchmark is constructed.

**Prefer short projects in the early phase.** A 12-month project started in Month 1 completes in Month 12 and frees both its budget slot and its capacity position for a new selection. Compounding this throughput across the 60-month horizon significantly increases the number of completions achievable. Reserve longer-duration projects for the middle phase of the run, when they can comfortably complete before Month 60.

**Slow proactively, not reactively.** The Cash Flow tab shows you forthcoming pressure months. A voluntary slowdown applied to a low-priority project two months before a projected shortfall is always less disruptive and less costly than a forced slowdown triggered by the shortfall panel. Treat the Cash Flow chart as a forward-looking management tool, not a historical record.

**Almost never abandon above 40% completion.** Once a project has consumed more than 40% of its budget, the sunk cost plus the two-point abandonment penalty will in almost all cases exceed the cost of completing it, even slowly. The principal exception is a very low-alignment project in the final months when budget is critically short and a near-complete high-alignment project needs rescuing.

**Speed-up is a rescue tool, not a productivity tool.** The crash premium makes acceleration expensive in absolute terms. It is justified only when a well-advanced project is projected to expire and its alignment is high enough that the completed delivery is worth the premium. Do not use it as a routine acceleration mechanism.

**Idle cash is not safety margin.** An unspent balance at Month 60 is penalised identically to abandoned project waste. If you approach the final months with substantial unspent funds and no new projects that can finish in time, consider voluntary speed-ups on near-complete active projects to pull their cash draw forward.

**Understand your political commitments.** If you are running with political projects enabled, identify them immediately, note their durations, and plan your discretionary portfolio around them. They cannot be removed until Month 24 and they will consume budget and capacity for their entire duration. Factor them into your cash flow projections from the first advance.

## 10  Glossary

**Annual Development Programme (ADP).** The annual capital budget of a provincial or federal government in Pakistan, covering physical infrastructure and development schemes across all sectors.

**Approval lag.** The period between a project receiving portfolio approval and it beginning to draw funds, reflecting the PC-I approval, tendering, and contracting process.

**BAC (Budget at Completion).** The total authorised budget for a project. In the simulation, BAC inflates monthly for unstarted projects and may be altered by risk events or speed-up premiums for active projects.

**Budget tightness.** The ratio of total project demand to available budget, determining how many projects can be completed. The standard setting of 1.5× means completing all projects would require 50% more than the available budget.

**Concurrent project cap.** An upper limit on the number of projects that may be simultaneously active or pending, representing the institutional capacity constraint on project supervision.

**Crash premium.** The additional cost applied to a project's remaining budget when a speed-up is applied, reflecting the higher unit cost of accelerated delivery.

**Earned Value Management (EVM).** A performance measurement methodology that integrates scope, schedule, and cost data to assess project progress. The simulation tracks cumulative nominal spend and cash drawn, which correspond to the EVM concepts of Budgeted Cost of Work Performed (BCWP) and Actual Cost of Work Performed (ACWP).

**Pending state.** The state of a project that has been added to the portfolio but is awaiting the expiry of the approval lag before beginning to draw funds.

**Planning and Development Board (P&DB).** The apex planning body of the Government of Punjab, responsible for ADP formulation, project appraisal, and portfolio oversight.

**Political project.** A project that has been committed through political or policy mandate rather than strategic merit assessment. In the simulation, political projects are low-alignment schemes forced into the portfolio at the start of the run and locked against abandonment for 24 months.

**Portfolio.** The full set of projects being managed together under a shared budget and governance framework.

**Release schedule.** The pre-determined pattern of quarterly budget tranches over the 60-month simulation period. The release profile (flat, S-curve, front-loaded, back-loaded, or volatile) is set at the start of each run and determines the timing and size of available funds throughout.

**Rebaseline.** The recalculation of a project's forward spending profile (S-curve) following a change in its remaining budget or duration. Triggered by slowdowns, speed-ups, resumptions after suspension, and risk events.

**Risk environment.** The overall intensity of project risk, set as a multiplier (calm, normal, turbulent) at the start of each run. Scales the probability and magnitude of cost and schedule shocks at project milestones.

**S-curve.** The characteristic shape of capital project expenditure over time — slow at the start, accelerating through construction, tapering as completion approaches. Derived in this simulation from a Beta(2,2) distribution.

**Strategic alignment.** The degree to which a project contributes to the government's development priorities, expressed as a score between 0.20 and 1.00. Fixed at project generation; not affected by financial changes during the run.
